History of Rhinoplasty

History of Rhinoplasty

Although rhinoplasty is not in its current form, it has been performed for thousands of years. It is described in the Edwin Smith Papyrus BC, when the nose correction operation was performed on individuals who could not breathe functionally, although not with aesthetic concerns. There are rumors that this Papyrus was written between 1500-1600 BC or 2500-3000 BC. Edwin Smith is the oldest known written surgical document.

Ebers Papyrus, also written in 1500 BC, named some of the operations performed on the nose as "plastic surgery". This ancient Egyptian papyrus tells us that rhinoplasty is performed to correct the nose of some people who have been subjected to a Rhinectomy (in cases of punishment or obligation). Encyclopedist Aulus Cornelius Celsus, who lived in the Roman Empire between 25 BC and 50 AD, described plastic surgery and rhinoplasty techniques in his 8-volume Encyclopedia De Medicina.

Approaching more modern times, in 1597, Italian Gasparo Tagliacozzi described the reconstruction of rhinoplasty with muscle and cartilage structures in his book "The Surgery of Defects by Implantations" and explained what should be done in 2-3 weeks after the operation.

In 1818, German Karl Ferdinand von Gräfe used the name rhinoplasty for the first time in his article "Rhinoplastic", written and published in Gentleman's Magazine, describing 55 different rhinoplasty operations.

At the beginning of the 20th century, more advanced methods were also introduced, rhinoplasty began to turn into a field of expertise, but Piezo Rhinoplasty, which was introduced by Massimo Robiony in 2004, is the most modern method used today.

Rhinoplasty, which is developing day by day with the advancing technology, is much more painless, and risk-free than before. Nowadays, patients can almost completely return to normal in their daily lives within 1-2 months after rhinoplasty operations.
